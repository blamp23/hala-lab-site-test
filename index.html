// what we study

Research

Our lab asks how environmental pollutants disrupt the molecular networks governing survival, reproduction, and development in Gulf Coast species — and uses computational modeling to predict those disruptions before they appear at the population level.

// 01 · Field & Analytical

Marine Toxicology

We measure legacy and emerging pollutants — PFAS, PAHs, PCBs, and microplastics — in surface waters and biota across northwestern Gulf of Mexico ecosystems including Matagorda Bay and Galveston Bay. We quantify metabolic and endocrine biomarkers of exposure to connect pollutant presence to physiological harm.

Southern Flounder Sheepshead Minnow Bottlenose Dolphin American Alligator
// 02 · Physiology

Vertebrate Endocrinology

We study how environmental stressors and chemical contaminants disrupt endocrine systems across vertebrate taxa — from early-stage zebrafish to adult American alligators. This comparative approach reveals shared mechanisms of hormonal disruption across wildly different animals.

Zebrafish American Alligator Southern Flounder
// 03 · Computational

Systems Biology & Metabolic Modeling

We apply constraint-based metabolic network modeling, flux balance analysis, and multi-omic data integration to predict how pollution rewires cellular metabolism. We build context-specific metabolic models from transcriptomic and metabolomic datasets to trace effects from molecular initiating events to physiological outcomes.

// where we work

Facilities

Wet Laboratory

Exposure systems and aquatic animal housing for toxicological experiments across marine and freshwater species. Equipped for acute and chronic bioassay studies with precise environmental controls.

Analytical Chemistry

Instrumentation for quantifying legacy and emerging contaminants in biological and environmental matrices. Capabilities include PFAS and PAH analysis across tissue, water, and sediment samples.

Computational Resources

High-performance computing access for constraint-based metabolic modeling, multi-omic data integration, and flux balance analysis using MATLAB, R, and the COBRA Toolbox.

// opportunities

We're Building the Next Generation of Environmental Scientists

The Systems Toxicology Laboratory welcomes motivated graduate students and postdoctoral researchers interested in marine toxicology, vertebrate physiology, and computational systems biology. We value curiosity, rigor, and a genuine interest in protecting Gulf Coast ecosystems.

Prospective PhD students should have a background in biology, biochemistry, environmental science, or a related field. Experience with MATLAB, R, or bioinformatics is a plus but not required — we train.

  • ✓Mentorship across wet lab and computational approaches
  • ✓Access to Gulf of Mexico field sites and collaborator networks
  • ✓Competitive funding through TAMUG fellowships and research grants
