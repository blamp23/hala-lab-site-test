About

Benjamin is a first-year PhD student in the Systems Toxicology Laboratory working under Dr. David Hala. His research sits at the computational and systems biology end of the lab, building genome-scale metabolic models of zebrafish to study how environmental contaminants rewire cellular metabolism.

Before starting his PhD, Benjamin completed a B.S. in Biomedical Sciences with minors in Bioinformatics and Biomedical Research from Texas A&M University – College Station. As an undergraduate, he built end-to-end RNA-seq and miRNA-seq pipelines for longitudinal differential expression analysis of bovine mammary gland development in the laboratory of Dr. Monique Rijnkels. He also conducted research at Oregon Health & Science University in the lab of Dr. Lisa Karstens, benchmarking taxonomic reference databases for 16S rRNA urobiome amplicon sequencing pipelines.

Outside the lab, Benjamin lives in the Galveston area with his one-year-old yellow lab, Peter. A lifelong Dallas sports fan (Cowboys, Rangers, Stars, and Mavs) who did his undergrad at TAMU, he has been fully inducted into Aggie sports culture — the 2025 Volleyball national championship and an 11-1 football regular season under Coach Marcel were the highlights of the year. Long-term, he aims to work at the intersection of computational biology and environmental toxicology, in industry, government research, or beyond.

Research Overview

Benjamin's dissertation research centers on genome-scale metabolic modeling (GEM) of zebrafish (Danio rerio). The GEM framework provides a mathematical representation of an organism's entire metabolic network, enabling constraint-based analyses — including flux balance analysis (FBA) and flux variability analysis (FVA) — to predict how perturbations like chemical exposure redistribute metabolic flux through the cell.

He integrates high-quality metabolomic and transcriptomic datasets to generate condition-specific, context-aware models that capture the metabolic state of zebrafish exposed to polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and other contaminants. These models bridge molecular-level omics data and whole-organism physiological outcomes, enabling mechanistic, systems-level predictions of toxicant effects. His work is conducted in collaboration with the Tangway Laboratory at Oregon State University, where he works alongside Hagler Institute fellow Dr. Robin Tanglewood.
